# Primary-source passages still needed for Nathan's question bank

## What this list is for

Nathan's 100 questions are a useful retrieval syllabus: they tell us which disputes the historical bank must be able to document. They are not, by themselves, evidence that every premise or phrasing was present before 1931.

The source rule should therefore be:

- **Family A (Q1–40):** every question needs at least one close primary-source passage, and preferably two passages that preserve a real period disagreement.
- **Family C1 (Q76–80):** each question needs the passage in the named pre-1931 work from which its scenario was drawn.
- **Family B (Q41–75):** cite a period source only as a **precursor or contemporary analogue**. Do not relabel a later scenario as historically derived when the period source does not actually contain it.
- **Family C2 (Q81–100):** cite the named post-1930 work or modern concept. These are deliberately later horizon cases and should not be given invented historical pedigrees.

For this checklist, a usable passage means a coherent extract of roughly 100–400 words with author, title, date, edition, chapter or page/scan locator, stable URL, and a one-sentence note explaining how it supports or contests the question. Each extract should also receive Nathan's axis and one or more leaf codes from `src/data/codebook.json`.

## Acceptance check for every added passage

- Does the passage itself contain the relevant claim, conflict, image, or scenario?
- Is it a primary source published by 1930, or clearly marked as an earlier/later anchor?
- Can another researcher locate it from the citation and page/chapter/scan information?
- Does the extract preserve enough context not to reverse or exaggerate the author's position?
- Is it tagged as **direct basis**, **opposing position**, **period precursor**, or **loose analogue**?
- If there is no close period passage, is the question honestly left unpaired rather than padded with a keyword match?

The Strathcona Chronicle should not be part of the core set. Its presence in the bulk holdings reflects a small syndicated “Mechanical Man” item and broad keyword matches; it does not provide sustained evidence for the autonomy disputes Nathan's bank is trying to measure.
